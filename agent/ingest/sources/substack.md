## What Izak Writes About

Izak is a Software Engineer in Denver (CS class of 2025, raised in Southern California) who writes across two distinct formats. The first is the lyric essay: literary, slow-burning, grounded in a specific physical moment — a train window, a bus ride, a windowless bedroom — and moving outward into something more philosophical without ever losing the intimacy of a first-person voice. The second is a weekly newsletter, launched in June 2026, that works more like a dispatch: a personal musing, what he read and listened to, what he ate, what stayed with him. Both formats share the same sensibility — an overthinker who processes the world by writing it down.
 
**Recurring themes:** Leaving before being left; the grief of unlived moments and unopened doors; carrying yourself wherever you go (the lesson that moving cities doesn't move your problems); analysis paralysis and Sylvia Plath's fig tree; emotional numbness and the search for feeling; identity in transition — who you are when the conventional script runs out; the unexpected comfort of a slow, routine life after years of intensity; shapeshifting to be loved; the strange terror of free will and self-authorship.
 
**Audience:** People in their early-to-mid twenties navigating post-college life — new jobs, new cities, new routines they didn't expect to find comforting. Readers who feel things deeply but have trouble trusting those feelings. People who overthink and overanalyze, who cook dinner and stay in most nights and are slowly making peace with that. People who have pressed rewind on a song six times trying to name something they can't quite say out loud.
 
**Cadence:** Two formats in parallel. The lyric essays are sporadic — a few per year, published when something has crystallized enough to be written. The weekly newsletter ("Izak's Newsletter") launched June 2026 and runs weekly, mixing a personal musing with media picks and food notes.

# Essay: Izak's Newsletter #1: That Darned Fig Tree
*Published: June 2026*

Hello!

I saw this idea (a newsletter of your life) maybe last year and I am finally acting on it. I realized that, while I think I live a quite boring life, I actually end up doing a lot of exciting things during the week—in my opinion, at least. I think a lot. And read, learn, observe, eat, etc., etc. This “newsletter” will be like a running dispatch from wherever I am, picking out what is vivid, what is strange, what stayed with me.

Honestly not sure what niche I am going to hit here, but I think an intro will help maybe form that. I graduated in 2025 with a degree in Computer Science and I am currently working as a Software Engineer in Colorado. A lot of my daytime is really just spent working, but in the afterhours, I like to cook + eat, read, and overthink + overanalyze.

Weekly Musings: That Darned Fig Tree

1 sitting in the sun, 2 sitting in the office, 3 sitting in the train, 4 my year of swag and resurrection
As I sat in the familiar heat of the Southern California sun earlier this week, I realized that I actually spend a considerable chunk of my time feeling paralyzed with possibility. I live a rather privileged life where I have the means to do pretty much everything I want to do, both at the large and small scale. On the grand scale, if I want to quit my job, pack up my life, and restart in a different state, I can do it. In the smaller scale, if I want to spend my Saturday morning hiking in the mountains, sleeping in, sitting in the park, or hanging out with friends—I have the ability to do all of these things.

The problem is that I cannot choose.

I constantly wade in an indefinite limbo, stuck in a loop, until I see myself sitting in the crotch of that darned fig tree, starving, watching the figs slowly wrinkle and rot on the vine then plop at my feet, and then I just feel even worse. Sometimes I wish that I was just a puppet, have someone tell me exactly what to do, so I do not have to dread afterwards if I made the right choice or not. Whether or not the fig that I ended up choosing was actually the sweetest one or if there was something else more ripe on the vine.

I am not going to pretend to have a resolution to this. “Analysis paralysis” is something that most people relate to and likewise feel unable to escape. A slightly freeing thought, I have found, is that life is actually not as mutually exclusive as I initially thought. Who said you can really only pick one fruit?

Pick a fig right now (”I will sleep in this morning…”) and pick another one after you have devoured and thoroughly enjoyed the first (”… then I will go sit in the park”).

Unremarkable, really, but slightly comforting :-)

Cheesman Park in the spring
Others musings:

I also have been writing something new. I am thinking of calling it “On Expectations”. Currently drawing inspiration from this Substack post “who are you when you’re not pleasing anyone?” and Joan Didion’s 1967 essay “Goodbye to All That”. Here is a short excerpt:

“What started as an ache to release became a torrent unstoppable. And in no time, the sky will become empty again, dry and barren, and it will wonder whether the weight was easier to bear than the emptiness that it now must carry.”

This quote from a hopecore Instagram Reel also stuck with me all week:

“You will never get what you want until you finally decide that you are deserving of it”

It could connect to the analysis paralysis issue that I mentioned above if you think about it…

Media of the Week (MOTW):

Reading: I finished reading Yellowface by R.F. Kuang. I would give it 3.5 stars. The plot is absorbing, but I did not like how the second half just turned into a sort of Twitter-thread retelling. After this I started reading “From Whom the Bell Tolls” by Earnest Hemingway and it is kind of boring so far. I might pick up something else.

I also rediscovered this awesome poem “Wild Geese” by Mary Oliver.

Music: Could not stop replaying “I Saw Your Face” by Malcolm Todd.

Food of the Week (FOTW):

1-4 Dija Mara, 5 Los Gallitos Taqueria, 6 Strawberry Cheese Danish

Visited my favorite Balinese restaurant back home, Dija Mara, and enjoyed every bit of it. I really recommend it if you find yourself in North County San Diego! Also had really good street tacos with Justin in Denver at this spot called Los Gallitos Taqueria. And lastly, I made some strawberry cheese danishes (recipe from the heart) and ate a lot of it while watching the Knicks v Spurs game two.

If you have made it this far—thanks! As I continue writing these entires, I hope to find out what I am really writing about because for now, who really knows what this is all about…

# Essay: Izak's Newlsetter #2: One Year Post-Grad
*Published: June 2026*

Today officially marks one year after graduating from undergrad.

My supa awesome grad photos that I love so much. Wow I am so unc.
In that time, four seasons came and went, I took a total of seventeen domestic flights within ten days of PTO, put twelve thousand miles on my car, received a total of six haircuts between Scott and Ken (zero from an actual barber), started one new job, ended my college job of four years, and made many, many new friends. Sure, a lot can happen in any given year, but a lot more than the usual definitely happened in this past twelve months. And with all this, fortunately, came also a wealth of lessons.

Some of these photos are not from the past twelve months, but that is alright, it is just for symbolic purposes.
After some reflection this past week, here are the three main lessons I learned in the past year that I will be holding onto for the near future.

Weekly Musings: Three Lessons
Lesson 1: “You take yourself with you wherever you go.”

In August, I packed up my life and drove halfway across the country to move from Los Angeles to Denver. Most people will tell you that your early twenties are the time to move out of your hometown. They reason this is the time to reinvent yourself, escape your past regrets, and begin anew.

I concur with their initial argument. It is the time to move (logistically, at least, having no kids or real responsibilities tying you down in one place), but I would disagree with the latter statement. What I have found is that you actually cannot escape your past regrets. No matter how many miles you move from home, how many times you try to shove your proverbial box of regrets with the skeletons in your closet, or how many versions of yourself you can create and reinvent—the truth is that you take yourself with you wherever you go.

If you are unhappy in Los Angeles, then you will be unhappy in New York, too. You might become distracted for a bit with the hustle and bustle of a new city, but in the quietness of your soul, whatever was there before, is still there now.

While my cross-country move was primarily motivated by a new job, I partly believed that I could also restart here. I thought that I could just leave behind all my mistakes by Memory Ln. in Los Angeles. As it turned out, Memory Lane is not addressed to a city, but to your own self.

Honestly, I am a work in progress here. Just because I have learned the lesson does not mean that I have mastered it. I am still trying to be okay with just being “Izak" and not a “California Izak” or “Colorado Izak”.

While it is true that a big part of who I am is who I am not anymore—meaning that outgrowing your past is a very natural process in the evolution of your identity—the deep essence of me is, well, still me.

Whether or not that is a good or bad thing, it is the truth.

I lowkey wrote about this already on “On The Road Again" published, here, on Substack, December 2025. Shameless self-promo :-)

Lesson 2: “What is there left to do?”

This one is a more practical lesson on goal setting and making sure that you remain ambitious. For those who were on the traditional four-year university track, we spent the last eighteen or so years trying to arrive at this point. We finished high school, attended university for four years, and now we have a full-time job. No more homework to submit, midterms to study for, or job applications to submit. Summit reached.

What is there left to do? Die?

During the first couple months of post-grad, I wondered the same thing. What is there left to do between now and the next big milestone? What even is the next milestone, marriage? A shiny job promotion? Having children? These are all true, but they are also so far away (at least for me).

What I have learned is that I needed to make new goals to work towards. Scary at first, but it is actually not too bad because I can come up with whatever and I can also set the deadline on my own. It can be as high stakes as possible or lower stakes if you need a break.

For me, I needed a real break after the grind that was the last four years. So while my goals were postmarked this year, they are not scheduled to be delivered for another year or two…

Sign up for that marathon this year… or next year! It does not matter when, but you should find something to work towards again now that you have “achieved it all”.

Lesson 3: “The desire for slowness.”

In a similar, somewhat contradictory note, I also have learned is that it is actually okay to live a slow and boring life. While attending college in the shimmery city of Los Angeles in my early twenties was a great privilege and experience, I noticed that that life really wore me out. Leaving it was the worst feeling. I dreaded not having a change of pace every ten weeks anymore. No more new faces, parties, random two A.M. excursions to the beach or an underground parking lot rave.

The dread remained and maybe even sharpened as I started working, when my days became unbelievably monotonous. Wake up, work, eat dinner, sleep, repeat. Tell this to a current college student and they might shiver in fear.

However, as the months passed, the dread began to dull out and the routine actually became a saving grace. For the first time in my life, I felt like I could breathe. No, I am just kidding, that is an exaggeration. But really, after years of constantly picking up and settling down—for once, there was less pressure to be or do something.

Instead of fearing the monotony, I learned to lean into it. I now love my self-proclaimed boring life. I go to work then I go home. Sometimes I host friends for dinner or sometimes I go out to eat, but most nights I just stay in. The raging inferno that was college life has reduced to a steady flame that keeps me warm and sane. “My hot blood’s been burning for so many summers now. It’s time to cool it down, wherever that leads.”

Who really knows, though? Maybe I just need some time to recharge and soon I will crave the unpredictability again, but for now, the slowness and domesticity of this season is doing me just fine.

There are so many other lessons that I have learned this past year, but these are just the three that stuck with me the most. The ones that I think about on the daily.

I would love to hear about what you have learned in your first year of post-grad if you are willing to share. Comment below or text it to me :-)

Media of the Week (MOTW):

1 My Year of Rest and Relaxation, 2 Obession, 3 The Lost Tour
Reads: I put down “For Whom the Bell Tolls” by Earnest Hemingway. Why read something that you do not want to? In place of it, I started reading “My Year of Rest and Relaxation” by Ottessa Moshfegh. I really enjoy it so far. I am a big fan of unreliable and hatable narrators. The whole concept is super interesting and I recommend it for anyone looking for a casual and ironically demented approach on existentialism.

Watches: On Tuesday, I watched the latest horror movie craze, “Obsession” directed by Curry Barker. The last couple of nights have been sleepless so it is safe to say that it is pretty scary. It stands out for its unique premise (for a horror film) and the suspense it is able to create with so little. Highly recommend watching in theaters while it is still showing.

Concerts: Does it count as MOTW that I got floor tickets to Phoebe Bridgers’ “The Lost Tour”? Not sure, but maybe I just want to brag. I got the Day Two presale slot, so I thought that there would be scraps left, but I entered the queue at the two-thousands and got my tickets easily. I thought since Denver (i.e. not a major, major city), there would be less demand. I re-joined the queue afterwards just to observe and I was in the thirty-thousands. Safe to say, I got lucky then.
